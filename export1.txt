Catalogued narrative justifications for limiting party sizes across fantasy worldbuilding
Catalogued narrative justifications for limiting party sizes across fantasy worldbuilding
Most stories that bother to justify this fall back on one of a few families of reasoning, and the strongest ones argue that more people would actually make things worse, not just fail to help.

Numbers don't address the actual problem. A lot of fantasy threats aren't the kind you beat by piling on bodies. A dragon, a lich, an eldritch thing, a god — throwing fifty people at it just produces fifty corpses. The bottleneck isn't manpower, it's some specific capability: a weapon only one person can wield, resistance to a curse, the bloodline that lets you touch the artifact, the skill to disarm the seal. Once the story establishes that the threat eats armies for breakfast, "bring more guys" stops being a solution. This is also the quiet logic behind the chosen-one trope — it exists partly to explain why you can't just substitute quantity.

More people are an active liability. This is the richest vein:

Logistics. A big group needs food, water, and a baggage train, moves slowly, and can't live off the land. A small team moves fast and stays fed. This is real military reasoning, not just hand-waving.
Stealth. Most quests are infiltration, not invasion. Fifty people can't sneak. The whole premise of the Lord of the Rings is that the Ring has to slip in unnoticed — march an army to Mordor and Sauron simply crushes it (and the proximity of that many ambitious people to the Ring is its own disaster).
Choke points and friendly fire. In a dungeon corridor only three people can reach the enemy anyway, so the other forty-seven are just standing in line. And a fireball doesn't check allegiances — area attacks make crowding genuinely dangerous.
Trust and secrecy. More people means more leaks, more potential traitors, more ways the villain's spies learn your plan. Sensitive missions want a small, vetted circle.
The world won't let you assemble that crowd. Often the constraint is baked into the setting: only a few suits of the needed armor exist, the portal admits a fixed number, the prophecy names specific people, the wards key to a small party, the corrupted zone is survivable only by those carrying the rare amulet. And practically, everyone competent is busy — the kingdom's soldiers are holding the border, and you can't strip the defenses for a single errand. The party is frequently just "who could be spared."

Corruption and escalation. Sometimes the goal itself is dangerous to be near — the Ring again, or treasure that drives people mad — so you minimize exposure. And on the strategic level, if you bring an army, the enemy brings a bigger one; the small deniable team is precisely the asymmetric move that breaks the stalemate of large forces.

One honest caveat: a lot of party-size limits in actual RPGs are really design reasons in disguise — turn-based combat gets unmanageable, screen space runs out, balance breaks. The in-fiction explanations above are often retrofitted onto that constraint. The well-written ones make the limitation feel inevitable; the lazy ones just never let you ask the question.

Real combat resolves far faster than RPG abstractions suggest — fast enough that the actual attacks are at the floor of what the human nervous system can even process. Here's the key reference point: simple visual reaction time is about 0.2 seconds (200 ms), and many committed attacks arrive in less time than that. That single fact drives everything below.

Fencing is the most precisely quantified, because the scoring machines measure it. The "lockout" window — how long after one hit the opponent can still register a hit — tells you how fast a touch resolves. In épée it's 40 milliseconds, foil 300 ms, and sabre 120 ms (sabre was raised back to 170 ms after the 2016 Olympics). The 40 ms épée figure is striking: it means two hits arriving 1/25th of a second apart count as simultaneous, because no human or referee can separate them. The committed attack itself — the lunge or flèche extension — lands in roughly 100–200 ms. 
Academy of Fencing Masters
Kiddle

Boxing. A jab reaches the target in about 0.1–0.15 seconds once thrown; one informal measurement clocked roughly 139 ms to throw a jab to the target. The fist itself moves at peak velocities around 9 m/s for amateurs (a bit higher, ~9.8 m/s, for trained rear-hand crosses), which works out to individual punch velocities of roughly 40–51 km/h. In a flurry, elite boxers throw 8–12 punches per second. Note the consequence: a jab arrives faster than you can react to it, so defense isn't reaction to the punch — it's reading the windup. That's why feints work. 
London Savate + 3

Kendo is less precisely documented, but the cut itself (men, kote, dō) is comparable — the strike phase is on the order of 100–200 ms once committed, with the whole engagement to a scoring cut often well under a second. The discipline's emphasis on ki-ken-tai-ichi (spirit, sword, body arriving as one) is essentially a demand that the whole strike collapse into a single instant.

So across all of them, the pattern is consistent: the actual strike is ~0.1–0.3 seconds, a complete exchange (engage → resolve) is maybe 0.5–2 seconds, and the attacks crowd right up against the ~0.2 s reaction-time floor.

Now the RPG comparison, which is where this gets interesting. A D&D combat round is 6 seconds, and in that round a character typically takes one action — one attack roll. But look at the real numbers: in 6 seconds a boxer could throw 30+ punches, and two fencers could complete several full exchanges. So a single RPG attack roll cannot represent one sword swing — there's no way a trained fighter swings once every 6 seconds. The roll is an abstraction over the entire round: dozens of feints, probes, parries, and footwork adjustments, with the die deciding whether any of that activity found a real opening. A "miss" isn't a whiffed blade; it's "nothing got through." This is why a 6-second round with one roll isn't unrealistic so much as zoomed out — it's compressing a second-by-second reality that's happening an order of magnitude faster than the dice acknowledge.

The systems that try to model real timing (initiative counts in fractions of a second, action-point systems, fencing-style priority) end up needing the kind of millisecond bookkeeping that fencing's scoring machines exist precisely because humans can't do it in real time.

You're right, and it's a better frame than the one I gave. The millisecond scale tells you why you can't react to a blow, but it tells you almost nothing about what a fight was as a lived event. The meaningful units are the exchange and the time-to-resolution — and once you zoom out to that scale, the gap between reality and the geek-media picture narrows a lot. But it narrows unevenly, and the split is the interesting part.

The crucial thing is to separate two clocks that the millisecond view collapses together: how long the decisive contact lasted, and how long the whole encounter took. They're wildly different numbers, and most of the popular confusion comes from conflating them.

The decisive contact was usually brutally short. With sharp weapons and no armor, one solid hit is catastrophic, so serious lethal exchanges tended to end in one or a few passes. HEMA dueling rulesets encode exactly this assumption: that most hits aren't disabling, but some — depending on where they land — are at least disabling or lethal, making any follow-up impossible. So the actual blade-on-blade resolution is closer to the fencing "touch" reality than to a five-minute choreographed duel. First blood or first deep thrust often was the fight. 
7 Swords

But the encounter could be long, because of everything wrapped around that decisive instant: circling, measuring distance, feinting, and above all waiting. When both fighters know a single mistake is fatal, neither rushes — they probe and bait, and most of the elapsed time is spent not in measure. This is why the millisecond layer actually feeds the slowness rather than contradicting it: precisely because a committed attack can't be reacted to, the rational play is to never commit until you've manufactured an opening. Caution is the child of speed. The medieval and Renaissance masters worry constantly about the double kill — the afterblow — for this exact reason: in the time your thrust takes to land, your dying opponent's thrust is already on its way. That fear is what stretches the encounter out.

Battlefield melee is where this two-clock structure is clearest, and it's well studied. Ancient and medieval battles routinely lasted two, three, even four or more hours, yet the consensus among combat-mechanics scholars (Goldsworthy, Sabin, Zhmodikov) is that the actual killing happened in short bursts. Serious hand-to-hand fighting probably couldn't last longer than about 15 minutes, and 5 minutes may be a generous average — pure exhaustion forbids more. So a battle was mostly lulls: after a few minutes the two sides backed away by mutual consent and necessity, with most of the battle spent in these pauses and only a small fraction in fierce contact. Fights decided in minutes were the exception, usually because one side broke and fled before real contact. And the attrition was slow: even in drawn-out engagements the winners rarely lost more than about 5 percent killed. That's a grinding war of nerves and fatigue, not a meat grinder. 
ResearchGate + 4

Then there's the case that aligns best with the media image: armored combat. Once both fighters are in plate, cuts and most thrusts simply don't work — so the fight devolves into wrestling, half-swording, grappling for a throw, and hunting with a dagger for the gaps (visor, armpit, groin). That genuinely is prolonged, physical, and back-and-forth, much closer to a wrestling match than a fencing touch. The "trade blows for a while until something gives" picture is real here — just not because they're parrying each other's swords ten times, but because the swords have stopped being the decisive tool at all.

So mapping it back to the geek/RPG model: the trope of the extended multi-exchange duel is false for the unarmored lethal case (which is fast and one-sided) but roughly true for battlefield and armored combat — and, notably, the RPG hit-point round structure accidentally models those two well. A pool of hit points whittled down over several rounds is a decent abstraction of the pulse-and-lull grind and the armored wrestling-match: the "damage" before the final blow stands in for fatigue, glancing hits, near-misses, and lost ground rather than literal sword wounds, and the killing blow only lands once the buffer is gone. That's why the systems that do aim at the unarmored-duel feel throw hit points out entirely and use one-hit-kill or death-spiral mechanics instead — they're modeling the other clock.

Your instinct is the right correction: the honest answer isn't "real fights are millisecond-fast" or "real fights are long like the movies." It's that the decisive moment is far faster than media shows, while the encanter around it is often longer and more hesitant than media shows — and which half the popular image got right depends entirely on whether anyone was wearing armor.

Synthesized biomechanics and game design to reconcile speed-strength dichotomy
Synthesized biomechanics and game design to reconcile speed-strength dichotomy
The cleanest thing I can tell you is that the speed-versus-strength dichotomy RPGs are built on doesn't really exist at the level where it matters — and seeing why it doesn't is what gives you the honest second axis you're reaching for.

Start with the physics, because that's the common point between the historical reality and the fantasy. A strike delivers kinetic energy, ½mv². There are two routes to a big number: raise v (velocity) or raise m (the effective mass you commit behind it). The fast character optimizes v; the strong character optimizes m. Because velocity is squared, the v-route is actually the more efficient one — which is exactly why a quick rapier thrust or a clean cross can be lethal with no bulk behind it. But here's the catch that collapses the dichotomy: to get the fist or the point moving fast you have to accelerate it, and acceleration is force applied over the strike's path (F = ma). High velocity at impact is itself a product of power. So "fast" already contains "strong" — just strength expressed as explosive acceleration rather than as a number on a barbell.

The physiology agrees. The fibre that makes you quick (fast-twitch, Type II) is the same fibre that makes you forceful and explosive, and both run on the anaerobic system you singled out. A genuinely fast person — a sprinter, an elite striker — is never weak; they're enormously powerful and have tuned that power toward velocity. The cartoon of the slow musclebound bruiser and the weak nimble duelist is, at the engine level, mostly false. The people who move fast are the people who can produce a lot of force quickly.

So your instinct is right, but I'd push it further: "strength" in the RPG sense is three different things wearing one mask, and only one of them is the lifting power you pictured. The first is explosive power — rate of force development, how much force you can pour out in the ~100 ms a strike lasts. You can't even reach maximal voluntary force in that window (that takes 300 ms or more), so a powerlifter's one-rep max is almost irrelevant to a punch. This is the thing that makes you hit hard, and it's shared with speed — same fibres, same anaerobic burst. The second is the gas tank: anaerobic capacity, how many explosive efforts you have before you empty out. This is the one you intuited as stamina, and it's the decider in drawn-out fights — recall the battlefield reality, where the limit on continuous combat wasn't courage but exhaustion, and the whole rhythm of pulses and lulls existed because nobody can stay explosive for long. The third — and only this one is "big muscles for lifting" — is grinding strength plus mass: slow, high-force, near-isometric output, and the body weight to anchor it.

That third kind is where raw strength stops being optional, and it answers "what would it mean to be fast but not strong enough." A fast, low-mass fighter is lethal before contact and helpless after it. The moment two swords actually clash and stay in contact — the bind — it becomes a near-static force contest (the German masters literally tracked the Stärke and Schwäche, the strong and weak of the blade, and "winding" is the technique of leveraging strength in the bind), and the stronger fighter shoves the line aside and owns the centre. The same with the clinch in boxing or the grapple with weapons — ringen am Schwert, wrestling at the sword. Grappling is almost pure strength-and-mass; you can't finesse your way out of being physically controlled by someone much stronger. So the fast-but-weak character's entire game is to win on the first tempo or never let the fight resolve into contact, because once a stronger opponent closes distance and binds or grabs, speed has nothing left to express. That's the historical fencer-versus-wrestler problem, and it's the same intuition you have when you imagine two swords clashing or two boxers leaning on each other on the inside.

So what do the muscles actually bring, staying anaerobic? Four things, and notice the first one: they make you faster. Explosive strength is what accelerates your own limbs and body, so it buys quicker strikes, a more violent lunge, a faster level change — speed and power aren't separable here. Second, effective mass and momentum behind whatever you throw, so your hits arrive with stopping power instead of just touching. Third, dominance in every contact phase — the bind, the clinch, the throw, and resisting the throw — plus the bracing and structure to not be moved and to absorb a hit without folding (and grip strength, quietly, for weapon retention and blade control). Fourth, the anaerobic capacity to keep all of that switched on through the few decisive seconds before the tank runs dry.

If you were redrawing the stats honestly from this, you'd get something like four axes rather than two: Power (explosive force — pays for both hitting hard and moving fast), Finesse (the neural layer — timing, accuracy, defense, reading the opponent; this is the real separable "fast," and it's the one thing on the list that isn't muscle at all), Gas tank (anaerobic capacity — wins the long encounter), and Frame (mass plus grinding strength plus durability — the real separable "strong," cashing out as control and grappling and not getting overwhelmed). RPGs blur these: DEX smears Finesse together with power-as-quickness, STR smears explosive power together with Frame, and a separate Constitution or stamina track is the system half-admitting that the gas tank deserved its own slot all along. Your suspicion that the second stat should be more stamina than lifting power is the system's own confusion surfacing — there was never one "other stat" opposite speed; there were three, and most games quietly fused them.

Synthesized real-world fighting archetypes across multiple disciplines and frameworks
Synthesized real-world fighting archetypes across multiple disciplines and frameworks
Both are true at once — and the reason they don't contradict is that they're two different axes, which is exactly the thing RPGs are quietly modeling when they separate level from build.

The quantitative thing you describe is real and it's dominant. Almost every physical attribute is positively correlated: the trained, well-fed, healthy, rested, well-equipped veteran is faster and stronger and has a bigger gas tank and reads tempo better than the starving conscript. There's something like a general athleticism factor, and on top of it sits skill, which is itself broadly correlated — good fighters are good at most things. This is the "is this actually a fighter or not" axis, and it swamps everything else. A novice with a perfect build loses to a competent generalist almost every time. In RPG terms this is the level/XP axis: the pump where all your numbers rise together, and the reason a high-level character beats a low-level one regardless of how either spent their points.

But archetypes are also unmistakably real, and they live on the axis that survives after you control for that. They emerge wherever something forces a genuine tradeoff, and three things reliably do.

Boxing is the cleanest case, because the archetypes are textbook and they form an honest rock-paper-scissors. The four classic styles — the out-boxer (range, jab, movement, win on points without getting hit), the swarmer/in-fighter (relentless pressure, close the distance, overwhelm), the slugger (low volume, devastating single power shots, accept getting hit to land one), and the boxer-puncher (the hybrid) — beat each other in a loop. The swarmer tends to overwhelm the slugger by smothering his power; the slugger tends to catch the out-boxer who stands at range; the out-boxer tends to pick apart the swarmer who has to walk in. These aren't cosmetic. They're dictated partly by choice and partly by physique you can't retrain: reach, height, hand speed, chin (how much you can absorb), and gas tank. A short, durable, high-output body is built to swarm; a tall, rangy, light-punching body is built to out-box. That's a character build in everything but name.

MMA makes the archetypes even sharper, because there are multiple ranges — striking, clinch, ground — and excellence in one trades against time spent on the others. The early UFC was pure style-versus-style: Royce Gracie's jiu-jitsu demolished strikers who had literally no answer on the ground. Hard counters, exotic matchups, wild build diversity. But here's the part that ties back to your worry: as the sport matured, the archetypes compressed. Once the counters to each pure style became known, every elite fighter had to shore up every weakness, and "MMA" converged into its own complete discipline. The pure grappler and the pure striker got squeezed out at the top in favor of the well-rounded fighter with a slight lean. Build diversity is highest in an unsolved metagame and collapses as the domain gets solved — which is a real and slightly melancholy law, and it's the same thing that happens to RPG metagames as players optimize toward a narrow set of "correct" builds.

Historical sword combat had the same structure. National schools were genuinely distinctive — the German Liechtenauer longsword tradition organized around seizing the initiative and winning the bind through winding; the Italian traditions; the Spanish Destreza with its circular, geometric footwork that looked almost alien to outsiders; the later, very different French smallsword game. And the weapon choice itself was a build: rapier, sidesword, longsword, sword-and-buckler each imply a different range and a different priority. But the lethal stakes pushed toward convergence too — masters were often prescriptive, claiming a "true art," because when a mistake kills you, idiosyncrasy gets selected out fast. So you get the same tension: real schools and real stylistic identity, under constant pressure toward whatever simply worked.

What keeps the build axis from dissolving into the level axis — the answer to "is it just all positively correlated" — is three sources of irreducible tradeoff. First, immutable physical givens: height, reach, limb proportions, frame, fast-twitch ratio. You can't train these away, and a short stocky fighter and a tall rangy one have genuinely different optimal games. Weight classes are the sport formally admitting this: mass is explicitly a tradeoff (power and durability against speed and gas tank), so undeniable that the entire enterprise is segregated by it. Second, finite training time: you cannot be world-class at everything simultaneously, so depth in one area is bought with shallowness in another — forced specialization. Third, temperament: risk tolerance, the pressure-fighter's appetite for exchanges versus the counter-puncher's patience, is partly psychological and creates style independent of attributes.

So the honest synthesis is that your intuition ("most things are positively correlated in a way that doesn't lend to builds") is correct about one of the two axes and that's precisely why good systems split them. The correlated pump is level; the orthogonal allocation that remains after you factor level out is the build. RPGs that feel realistic are the ones that respect both — a high-level generalist should beat a low-level specialist, and two equally skilled fighters should still play rock-paper-scissors by archetype. The genres that flatten it into one number (just "combat skill") lose the archetype texture you care about; the ones that flatten it the other way (pure builds, no level dominance) produce the unrealistic outcome where a clever low-level build beats a veteran.

And there's a worldbuilding payoff hiding in the maturation point: vivid archetypes are a feature of unsolved fighting worlds. A fantasy setting full of exotic, half-mastered disciplines is, metagame-wise, the early UFC — which is exactly why it naturally supports more distinct, flavorful builds than a hyper-optimized modern sport ever could. The thing you want as a player isn't unrealistic. It's realistic for a world that hasn't yet been solved.

Synthesized four-stat wound system with commitment mechanics and death spiral
Here's a system that falls directly out of everything above. The design goal is that the mechanics produce the realities we described rather than bolting them on: the fight should be mostly cautious probing punctuated by a sudden lethal burst, one clean hit should be able to end it, committing should risk the double-kill, getting tired should change who you are, and the four-way stat decomposition should generate the rock-paper-scissors archetypes on its own.

The stats. Keep the four axes we landed on, plus one extra that does the level-vs-build work from the last message.

Power — explosive force. Does double duty exactly as the physics demanded: it adds to your tempo (who acts first) and to your wound severity when you land. This is "fast already contains strong."
Finesse — the neural layer. The main modifier to the core roll when fighting at range: timing, accuracy, defense, reading.
Gas — anaerobic capacity. A draining pool, not a bonus. It's the clock.
Frame — mass, grinding strength, durability. Two jobs: it soaks incoming wound severity, and it replaces Finesse as the governing stat once the fight goes to contact (the bind/clinch).
Grade — the correlated pump. One number bundling experience + health + nutrition + equipment, added to almost every roll and to wound resistance. This is the thing that's "positively correlated and doesn't lend to builds" — so it's deliberately not a build choice. Two fighters of equal Grade play rock-paper-scissors; a higher-Grade fighter mostly just wins. Build is how you split points among the four stats; Grade is the tide that lifts all of them.
So a character is: a Grade (say 0–3), and a build (allocate ~8 points across the four stats, each 1–5).

The core roll is your "skill modulated by luck." Each exchange, both fighters roll a die, add the governing stat and Grade, and compare. The margin is what matters, not just who won. I'd default to 2d6 + stat + Grade, because the bell curve makes skill the spine and luck the wobble — but that's your variance knob: swap to 1d12 and luck can upset skill (the lucky peasant kills the knight occasionally); go to 3d6 and skill almost always tells. Pick the d12 if you want a world of dramatic upsets, 3d6 for a grim world where the better fighter wins.

Two choices drive the whole thing, and they are the historical tension:

Range. The fight has three distances — Out of measure, At measure, In the bind (contact). At measure, the core roll is governed by Finesse. In the bind, it's governed by Frame. Each exchange you declare whether you're trying to close, hold, or open distance; if the two fighters disagree, that's its own quick opposed roll (Power for the explosive closing, Finesse to deny it). This single rule produces the spatial rock-paper-scissors: the light fast fighter is lethal at range and helpless once bound; the strong fighter wants to drag it into contact where speed has nothing to express.

Commitment. This is the afterblow and the lull, the heart of it. Each exchange you fight Measured or Committed.

Measured (the lull): the winner can't inflict more than a Graze, but it's safe — and you may bank a Read (+2 next exchange) or recover a little Gas. This is the circling, probing, baiting. You cannot win the duel this way; you can only set it up.
Committed (the pulse): the winner can land anything up to a Kill — but if the margin was thin (say ≤2), the afterblow triggers: the loser, dying, still lands one tier lower. That's the double-kill the masters feared. And if you commit and lose, you're exposed: severity against you is bumped up a tier.
The emergent behavior is the real one: nobody wants to commit until they've manufactured an edge — a Read, a tempo win, or an opponent low on Gas — so duels are long stretches of Measured probing broken by one decisive Committed burst. You didn't script the caution; the afterblow rule made caution the rational play. Caution is the child of speed, mechanically.

Wounds instead of HP. When someone lands, compute severity = margin + Power − (defender's Frame + Grade), and read off a tier: ≤0 deflected, 1–2 Graze, 3–4 Wound, 5–6 Grievous, 7+ Kill. A hit to a vital location (roll or call it) bumps a tier. The key is that wounds are a death spiral, which is what makes one clean hit able to end the fight without a hit-point slog: a Graze is −1 to all your rolls, a Wound −2 and it drains Gas faster, a Grievous −4 and starts a bleed clock. Penalties stack and apply to your dice — so the first solid hit tilts every subsequent exchange against you, and the fight accelerates toward a conclusion instead of grinding. That's "the decisive contact is short and brutal" expressed as positive feedback.

The Gas clock is what stops the probing from going forever and what makes exhaustion change who you are. Gas drains every exchange — a little when Measured, more when Committed, more per wound you're carrying, and (the nice realistic sting) more the bigger your Frame, because mass burns oxygen. When Gas drops past a threshold, you take a penalty to Finesse and Power — you literally slow down and hit softer. So the strong pressure-fighter who can't finish fast enough becomes a different, worse fighter in the late exchanges, and the fast fighter who couldn't land the kill before gassing is in the same trouble. The clock is why you must eventually commit.

Watch the archetypes fall out of the build, unscripted. A high-Finesse, low-Frame, decent-Gas build naturally plays the out-boxer: stay at measure, win Measured exchanges, never let it go to the bind. A high-Frame, high-Gas build plays the swarmer: force the bind where Frame governs, spend the opponent's Gas, win the late game — and it beats the slugger but gets picked apart by the out-boxer it can't catch. A high-Power, low-Gas build is the slugger living for one Committed kill-shot, lethal to the out-boxer and smothered by the swarmer. Nobody assigned these; they're just where the math is comfortable, which is exactly what an archetype is.

Two optional modules that plug straight into the earlier history: armor flips Frame's meaning — cuts and thrusts mostly deflect, so striking severity is gutted and the fight is forced into the bind/grapple, making Frame king and turning the duel into the wrestling-match reality of armored combat. And weapon choice just sets your default range and which distances you're comfortable contesting — the rapier is a build that wants to live At measure, the dagger a build that only works in the bind.

If you want, I can turn this into a one-page playtest sheet with the exact Gas numbers, a wound table, and a worked example exchange between two of the archetypes — easier to actually roll against than prose. But I'd test the core loop first: two equal-Grade fighters, a few Measured exchanges, then someone commits. If that single sequence feels like a duel, the rest is tuning.

Every one of your questions is really "which bucket does this go in," and the buckets are the interesting part.

The cleanest frame is to separate what you are from what you know from what you carry — three independent tracks that grow at different rates and from different sources. Most RPG incoherence comes from blending them; keeping them apart makes each of your questions almost answer itself.

Training as its own track is the one to commit to hardest, because it's the idea you opened the whole conversation with — "is this actually a fighter or not" — and it deserves to be mechanically distinct from raw attributes. Remember the level-vs-build split from way back: the correlated pump (experience, knowing how to fight at all) versus the orthogonal allocation (your DEX/STR shape). Training is that pump, and it should be a flat, broad competence bonus that applies regardless of weapon — the thing that separates a veteran from a conscript even when their bodies are identical. This is also where weapon-specific training lives, as a second, narrower layer on top:

General combat training — a flat bonus to all tempo rolls. "You know how to fight." Cheap-ish to start, expensive to max. This is your veteran-vs-novice axis.
Weapon proficiency — a per-weapon-class bonus (blades, hafted, polearms, bows…) on top of general training. This is what makes a master swordsman specifically a swordsman, and it creates a real switching cost: pick up an unfamiliar weapon and you keep your general training but lose the proficiency layer. That cost is the whole point — it's what makes a build a commitment rather than a loadout you swap per fight.
That gives you a satisfying answer to "trained combatant vs trained in a specific weapon": they're two stacked tracks, broad and narrow, and the narrow one is where weapon identity and specialization tension live.

On weapons themselves — the trap is making them flat upgrades (the longsword is just better than the shortsword). What you want is for each weapon to interact with your three combat stats differently, so that weapon choice is build expression, not a power ranking. How it weights the tempo roll — does it favor DEX (a rapier rewards the precise fighter) or let STR contribute (a greatsword)?

Its STA cost per swing — heavy weapons burn the clock faster, which ties directly into the burst/sustain loop. The greataxe is a burst weapon mechanically, not just thematically: it gasses you out, so it wants a high-STA frame behind it.

The elegant result: a weapon isn't strong or weak, it's suited or unsuited to your build. A greataxe in a glass-cannon's hands is a liability (drains the STA they don't have); in a high-STR/high-STA frame it's terrifying. That's the rock-paper-scissors logic from the matchup loop, now applied to gear.

Stat growth — your "genetic but should be raisable" tension — has a clean resolution: let the thing that grows be different from the thing that's fixed, even if they feed the same number. Realistically DEX/STR are largely genetic; the fun-vs-realism fix is to say the cap is your genetic potential (fixed at character creation, and this is where heroic vs human vs monster ranges live) while training raises you toward that cap. So growth is real and earned, but a human can't train into a giant's STR — they're climbing toward their own ceiling, not an infinite ladder. This also gives you two distinct progression feelings: the slow, hard, expensive climb of a core attribute (toward a fixed personal limit) versus the faster, broader accumulation of training and proficiency. Players feel both "I'm getting tougher" (attributes, rare) and "I'm getting better" (skills, frequent), which is a richer curve than one XP bar pumping everything.

A worldbuilding bonus falls out of this for free: rare items or magic that raise the cap itself become genuinely special — a potion that lifts your STR ceiling is far more meaningful than one that just hands you +1, because it expands what you could ever become.

Armor is where you can do something better than HP-padding, and your lethal wound system makes it possible. Don't let armor add HP or just reduce damage by a flat number — that's the boring version. Armor's real job, historically and mechanically, is to shift the wound tier, which plugs directly into your severity-to-tier table:

Light armor — leave tempo untouched; maybe soak 1 point of severity. The duelist's choice: stay fast, accept that a clean hit still hurts.
Heavy armor — knock incoming severity down a tier or two (a Grievous becomes a Wound, a Wound becomes a Graze), but at a cost that's also expressed in your existing stats: a DEX penalty to the tempo roll and higher STA drain (the weight tires you). So heavy armor literally trades your speed and your clock for survivability — which is the exact tradeoff real armor imposes, and it slots into the burst/sustain loop without a new subsystem.
And the callback to the armored-combat discussion from earlier: heavy armor making cuts/thrusts mostly bounce is literally the tier-shift mechanic — against a fully-armored target, most hits drop to Graze or Deflected, which is what pushes those fights toward grapple and the STR/contact game. Armor doesn't just protect; it changes what kind of fight it is. You can even let certain weapons (a warhammer, a pick) ignore the tier-shift — the historical answer to plate was a weapon that didn't try to cut it — which gives you a beautiful gear-counters-gear layer on top of the build-counters-build one.

So the high-concept spine: three tracks (attributes climbing toward fixed genetic caps; training as a broad-plus-narrow skill layer; gear as build-suited rather than ranked), with armor shifting wound tiers at the cost of DEX and STA, and weapons weighting your three stats differently so every piece of gear is an expression of build rather than a rung on a ladder. XP's job is to buy the middle track freely and the first track rarely — and never the cap.

The one decision I'd nail down before anything else: is weapon proficiency tied to a weapon class (broad, forgiving) or individual weapons (narrow, punishing)? That single choice sets how much your combat identity locks you in, and everything else about weapons calibrates off it.

This is more settled than it feels to you — and the thing making you feel inconsistent is actually a clean rule you haven't named yet. Let me reflect that back, flag the four real decisions, then hand you the organized plan.

The principle that dissolves the "inconsistency." You're not torn between simulation and gameyness — you're drawing a layer boundary: simulate inside the fight, go gamey between fights. Your stat intuition is right, not inconsistent. STR/DEX/STA are the simulated body, so they're fixed — you can't train your way to a giant's strength any more than you can in life. Skills, levels, gold, and loot are the game layer, so they're freely allocated and unrealistic on purpose. And magic is the membrane between the two: the in-world license for gamey effects to reach into the simulated body (stat boosts, mid-fight saves). That gives you a decision rule for every future "sim or game?" call: does it happen during the fight, or around it? During → lean sim. Around → lean game.

With that, the genuinely open decisions are only four:

What each currency buys (your gold question). The cleanest non-overlapping split: XP/levels → permanent ability (skills + proficiencies); gold → consumables, ammo, trash gear, rest/services (the survival/breadth layer); loot & quests → the cool weapons + stat transcendence (the narrative power spikes). The thing to protect: never let gold buy permanent combat ability or the Named weapons, or it collapses into XP and loot and the economy goes flat. Gold buys staying power, not power.
Are INT and CHA fixed like the body stats? Recommend yes — all five fixed at creation, one clean rule. INT then scales magic the way STR scales weapons. CHA is the odd one: it never acts inside a fight, it lives entirely in the meta/party layer (companions, the disengage/quest choices). So: five stats, all fixed; STR/DEX/STA drive combat, INT drives magic, CHA drives the party layer.
Weapon proficiency granularity + guns. With only five curated weapons, per-individual-weapon proficiency is right — the usual reason to bucket into classes (too many weapons) doesn't apply, and per-weapon makes the switching cost a real commitment. And guns want ammo as a gold-bought consumable: a natural balancing lever and the one weapon that ties into the economy. Armor. Your only genuinely undeveloped system.
